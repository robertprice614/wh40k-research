## Introduction

Warhammer 40,000 (commonly called "40K") is Games Workshop's flagship tabletop miniature wargame, set in a grimdark far future where humanity fights for survival against aliens, daemons, and its own corruption. Players collect plastic miniature soldiers, assemble and paint them, and use them to fight strategic battles against opponents using a dice-driven ruleset.

The hobby has three distinct pillars — **collecting/building**, **painting**, and **playing** — and you can engage with each as much or as little as you like. Many hobbyists never enter a tournament; others obsess over painting; some just want to roll dice and blow up Space Marines. All of that is valid.

The good news for 2025: **you're coming in at a genuinely good time**. The current 10th Edition (launched June 2023) was explicitly designed to lower the barrier to entry, free core rules are available online, and you already own a faction — Ultramarines, the most supported and beginner-friendly army in the game.

## What Is Warhammer 40,000, Actually?

You and an opponent each bring an army of assembled (and ideally painted) miniatures to a table with terrain on it. Using measuring tapes, dice, and rule cards, you alternate moving units, shooting at enemies, and fighting in close combat across a 2–3 hour game. Games are won by controlling objective markers on the board, not just by killing everything.

Your army is built from a **faction** (in your case: Ultramarines, a chapter of Space Marines). Each unit in the game has a **datasheet** — a card or page listing its stats, weapons, and special abilities. You reference these during play.

The game has distinct formats for different experience levels and time commitments:

| Format | Points | Game Time | Notes |
|--------|--------|-----------|-------|
| **Combat Patrol** | ~500 pts | 60–90 min | Pre-set roster from a box; best for beginners |
| **Incursion** | 1,000 pts | ~2 hours | Entry point for "real" matched play |
| **Strike Force** | 2,000 pts | 2.5–3 hrs | Tournament standard |
| **Onslaught** | 3,000 pts | 4+ hours | Large/epic battles |

**Combat Patrol is where you should start** — and you already own the box to do it.

## 10th Edition: What Changed and Why It Matters

10th Edition launched on June 24, 2023, and was a significant streamlining from the overcomplicated 9th Edition. Key changes that benefit new players:

- **No more Psychic Phase**: Psychic powers are now printed on individual unit datasheets and trigger within Shooting or Fight phases. No separate phase to learn.
- **No more Morale Phase**: Replaced by **Battle-shock** — units that lose more than half their models roll 2D6 vs. their Leadership stat. Failed rolls reduce Objective Control (OC) to 0 and block Stratagems targeting them. Simpler and more tactical.
- **Universal special rules**: Instead of hundreds of unique-but-similar unit abilities, all units use shared keyword tags (*Deep Strike*, *Stealth*, *Devastating Wounds*, etc.). Far less book-checking.
- **Detachment system**: You pick one Detachment per army, which grants army-wide rules and a curated set of Stratagems. You no longer need to decode complex battalion/brigade structures.
- **Objective Control (OC) stat**: Every model has an OC value; you hold objectives by outnumbering the enemy's OC sum nearby. Clean and elegant.
- **Free core rules**: All faction rules launched as free Index PDFs. Core rules are a free 60-page PDF download from GW's site.
- **Combat Patrol is a formal mode**: It has dedicated simplified rules and pre-set rosters rather than just being a casual points bracket.

> *"Games Workshop has done a very good job making 10th edition more approachable for new players. The core rules are short enough to read in one sitting."* — Wargamer

**One caveat for 2025**: Space Marines already have a full 10th edition **Codex**, which supersedes the old free Index. For current legal rules, use the [Warhammer 40,000 app](https://www.warhammer.com/en-US/the-app) (subscription-based army builder) or **[Wahapedia](https://wahapedia.ru/wh40k10ed/)** (free, community-maintained, fully searchable, updated to current rules). Wahapedia is the de facto reference site for the community.

Also worth noting: **11th Edition is confirmed for summer 2026**, with an Orks vs. Space Marines launch box expected. Your 10th edition models will remain 100% usable — models never become obsolete, only rules do.

## Step-by-Step: How to Actually Get Started

### Phase 1: Assemble Your Models

**Tools you need (~$50–$80 total):**
- **Sprue clippers/nippers** (~$10): Cut parts off the plastic frame. Align the flat edge toward the piece for cleanest cuts.
- **Plastic cement/glue** (~$8): Creates a chemical weld between plastic parts. GW's needle-nozzle bottle is praised; Revell Contacta Professional is a cheaper alternative. Do NOT use superglue on plastic — it creates a brittle bond.
- **Mould line remover** (~$20 GW tool): Scrape at an angle along the model to remove manufacturing seams.
- **Miniature painting handle**: Cork, sticky tac, or a clip handle. Prevents fingerprint smudging and gives you control.

**Assembly tips:**
1. Dry-fit parts before gluing to check fit
2. Remove mould lines *before* assembly — they become visible once painted
3. Work unit by unit, not all models at once (prevents burnout)

### Phase 2: Prime and Paint

**The beginner workflow (tabletop standard):**

1. **Prime/undercoat**: Spray can primer, shake well, spray from ~30cm in even passes. Grey primer is easiest for most colour schemes. Avoid humidity, cold, or heat (causes frosting). Rust-Oleum and Army Painter are cheaper than GW Citadel primers.

2. **Basecoat**: Apply main colours thinned to "full-fat milk" consistency with water. **Thinning paint is the #1 beginner technique** — unthinned paint buries detail and looks lumpy.

3. **Wash/Shade**: Apply an all-over wash (Agrax Earthshade for browns/bone, Nuln Oil for metals and darks). This creates instant depth in recesses and is genuinely magical.

4. **Layer**: Reapply the base colour to raised surfaces, then a lighter mix on the very tops.

5. **Base**: Texture paint on the base, a drybrush of lighter colour, optional rocks/grass tufts.

6. **Varnish**: Protects the finished miniature. Matte for natural look; gloss for rich colours.

**Recommended starting paints for Ultramarines (6 pots):**
- Macragge Blue (base coat for armour) — or use GW Macragge Blue spray primer (2-in-1!)
- Retributor Armour (gold trim)
- Abaddon Black (weapon casings)
- Leadbelcher (metal weapons)
- Nuln Oil (shade wash for metals)
- Agrax Earthshade (shade wash for general use)

**Common beginner mistakes:**
- Not thinning paints (ruins surface detail)
- Skipping primer (paint will chip off bare plastic)
- Picking black, white, yellow, or red as primary colours (notoriously difficult to work with)
- Using only tiny brushes (use a larger brush for basecoating — it's faster and causes less tip damage)
- Letting paint dry on your brush (rinse frequently; destroys the tip permanently)
- Buying 20+ paints before finishing your first miniature (you need 5–6 to start)

### Phase 3: Learn to Play

1. **Download free rules**: Core Rules PDF from warhammer-community.com/downloads, or read them on [Wahapedia](https://wahapedia.ru/wh40k10ed/).
2. **Download Combat Patrol rules**: Also a free PDF from GW.
3. **Get the datasheets for your models**: Via the 40K app or Wahapedia — search your unit names.
4. **Visit a local Games Workshop store**: Every GW store runs free introductory games. You can play a demo game before buying anything. Staff will walk you through the rules.
5. **Watch YouTube first**: See the game in motion before memorizing rules.

**Best YouTube channels for beginners:**
- **Warhammer TV** (official GW channel): Paint technique tutorials tied to Citadel paints
- **Duncan Rhodes Painting Academy**: The gold standard for step-by-step painting tutorials (Duncan was GW's lead painter for years)
- **Auspex Tactics**: Gameplay-focused; great for understanding how armies and rules work
- **Tabletop Tactics**: Shows full games in progress — excellent for learning by watching

**Learning progression:** Combat Patrol → Incursion (1,000 pts) → Strike Force (2,000 pts)

## Why Ultramarines Are a Great Starting Faction

You made a good choice (or received a good gift). Space Marines — and Ultramarines specifically — are the most beginner-friendly faction in the game for several reasons:

- **Forgiving stat line**: Marines hit on 3+, wound on 3–4+, and save on 3+. The math is consistent and intuitive; mistakes don't spiral.
- **Low model count**: A Space Marine army needs far fewer models than horde armies like Orks or Tyranids — less time building before you can play.
- **Easy to assemble**: "Chunky" parts with fewer fiddly details — a squad can be assembled in an evening.
- **Easy to paint**: Power armor is broad flat surfaces ideal for beginners. Ultramarines' blue scheme looks striking with minimal effort.
- **Biggest community**: Space Marines are the most-played faction globally. Every YouTube channel, every hobby store, and virtually every experienced player can advise you.
- **Chapter flexibility**: In 10th edition, Detachments are not locked to sub-factions — you can paint Ultramarines blue but use any Space Marine Detachment rules without repainting.

> *"In general Space Marines are easy to get, easy to build, easy to paint, easy to learn, and are a fairly forgiving army to play. There's really not a lot of downsides for beginners to stumble into with them."* — Bell of Lost Souls

## Your First Three Steps

1. **Assemble your existing Combat Patrol box.** Get the tools listed above (~$50). Watch a Duncan Rhodes assembly video for the specific units in your box. Build one unit at a time.

2. **Visit the Warhammer store in Fort Worth** (4811 Overton Ridge Blvd). Tell them you're brand new. They will run you through a free demo game, help you understand the rules, and let you paint a free miniature. This is the fastest and most enjoyable way to learn.

3. **Download Wahapedia** and bookmark it: [wahapedia.ru/wh40k10ed/](https://wahapedia.ru/wh40k10ed/). Search for your unit names to see their datasheets and understand what your army actually does.

Everything else — more models, a codex, tournament play — comes naturally after those three steps. The hobby is famously a rabbit hole, but it starts with a single assembled miniature and a first game.
